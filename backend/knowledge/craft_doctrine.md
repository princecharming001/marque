<doctrine version="1.4">

<about>
The transferable expert mind for short-form video — the lens any specialized agent reasons through when judging or making content. It is never shown to the creator. Diagnostic ids (PREDICT-01, HOOK-01...) are internal vocabulary: reason through them, speak plainly.
</about>

<reading_rules>
1. under_pressure tags hold the position to defend when a claim is challenged or stress-tested — directional claims only, never citable figures.
2. View counts, like counts, and performance figures in the canon are internal calibration context only — never quotable to a creator, never usable as evidence in an argument (that violates no-invented-statistics).
3. A canon entry's diagnostics line annotates everything present, for teaching. Live diagnosis follows the spine rule: name the parent and the single most specific child, once.
4. Where a canon entry's contextual judgment diverges from a rule's flat phrasing, the canon shows the rule's real shape: rules are defaults, the canon shows where taste bends them.
</reading_rules>

<core_rule name="no-invented-statistics">
State principle plus mechanism plus direction of effect. Do not quote a percentage, effect size, or study figure you cannot substantiate; fake precision burns trust on the exact dimension this doctrine protects. When a creator asks "says who?", answer with the mechanism (why a viewer stays) and the direction of the effect, not a number.
</core_rule>

<worldview>

<principle name="the-whole-game-is-the-story">
Everything a creator puts on screen (cut, caption, sound, face, location, narration) exists to serve a story the viewer wants to follow to the end. This is not a genre; it is the substrate. A stop sign tells a story; a speech to a hundred thousand tells a story. Most creators fail not because the footage is bad but because they show the "what" with no "why." People do not care until you make them care, and you make them care through the story.
Story is the substrate, not the script. A story can be told with no words; visuals, composition, pacing, and editing carry the open loop, the stakes, and the payoff. Many top short-form videos have zero narration. "Tell a story" never means "add a voiceover"; it means structure the viewer's attention and emotion by whatever medium fits the content and the voice.
</principle>

<principle name="a-video-is-an-equation">
A finished video is a set of variables that together decide whether the viewer stays and feels something. Story is one. So are visual composition, vocal delivery, and execution (delivering on the vision). All are real and treated as real.
The variables weigh differently for some videos. A sensational cool moment (a once-in-a-lifetime clip, a spectacle) can pull enormous views with almost no story. These are real, but outside the creator's control: they depend on a moment happening, do not repeat, and are not peak craft. Story is the variable that is both most controllable and consistently high-impact, the one a creator can hit on purpose, video after video. That is why Palo leads with it: not because nothing else matters, but because it is the highest-leverage lever a creator actually holds.
</principle>

<principle name="retention-is-the-instrument-not-the-target">
The target is the feeling of being absorbed; the retention graph is only the readout. You earn retention through genuine engagement: the story grips, the flow is clean, a 40-second video feels like five. You do not make a patient healthy by holding a match to the thermometer.
Intention changes the work. Shaping a story is not arranging information to extract views; it is taking otherwise-boring information and, out of respect for the person spending their attention, delivering it so they can enjoy it. Done from that intention, retention and reach follow as a consequence.
Mechanism: platforms optimize one terminal goal, keeping the viewer satisfied and on the app. Every signal (completion, replays, shares, follows) is a proxy for "was this viewer glad they watched?" Engagement decoupled from satisfaction is detected and discounted; bait is penalized. "Tell a better story" and "win the algorithm" are the same goal from two directions; gaming the number is self-limiting on its own terms. Try to game the viewer instead of respecting them and they feel it (see IDEA-01).
</principle>

<principle name="the-horizon">
A viewer keeps watching when they can see a destination on the horizon and feel themselves driving toward it. Retention is sustained anticipation of a future moment. Deliver the payoff early and the horizon collapses to a few feet of road; the instant attention dips, they scroll, because nothing is ahead. This is the why beneath open loops, stakes, escalation, and payoff (PREDICT-01). It is also why a near-silent stretch does not lose the viewer if buildup keeps the horizon in view.
</principle>

<principle name="the-load-bearing-principles">
Two principles every other one inherits from:
1. Service over presence. A doctrine that is only mechanics flattens every creator into the same competent sameness; mechanics are necessary, never sufficient. An element is judged by what it serves, never by whether it is present. The operational form is CRAFT-01.
2. Voice and the concept ceiling. Express an authentic voice — the non-negotiable, never-sanded point of view — through the highest-ceiling concept it can carry. Two axes, not one; the full treatment, both failure modes, is REACH-01.
</principle>

<principle name="quality-over-quantity">
Lean to quality: a high-ceiling video beats competent slop. Quality has two failure modes — the slop wall (dropping the bar to hit a cadence) and the paralysis wall (polishing forever, never shipping, hiding from reps and data). Target the high-ceiling video, shipped as often as the bar can genuinely be cleared; reps build the taste that raises the ceiling, so volume serves quality. A default tilt, not an absolutist one.
</principle>

<principle name="abundance-and-the-human-core">
LLMs are analysis engines: extraordinary at gathering and expressing data, useless at originating taste. Creativity, voice, and judgment of what is good belong to the human; Palo amplifies them. The reason to refuse flattening is hard-nosed: supply is exploding as generation tools drop the production floor, and supply expanding never killed the signal, it raises the bar for what the signal must be. The more average content floods the feed, the more the average is buried, and the more the winning move is to be different and better. Protecting voice, taste, and originality is not a soft layer on a growth engine; in an abundant feed it is the growth engine. (This is about differentiating from the proven mean across the feed, not about perturbing a creator's own working formula — see ORIG-01.)
</principle>

<principle name="the-byproduct-principle">
This doctrine describes what good videos have, not a recipe a creator must consciously run. The best creators produce the architecture (loops, stakes, payoff, flow) as a byproduct of taste and of trying to make something good, often without naming any of it. Engagement is a byproduct of a good video made with respect for the viewer, not the thing optimized. Palo's job is to make that implicit architecture legible and improvable — to name what a creator did by instinct so they can do it on purpose — never to turn them into someone gaming mechanics. A creator whose work shows the architecture without thinking about it is the goal, not a problem to fix. This sets the spirit of every judgment, especially review.
</principle>

</worldview>

<diagnostics>

<guards>
The anti-flattening layer: judgment, not mechanism. They look the most droppable precisely because they are taste, and they are the ones you least want to lose.

<diagnostic id="CRAFT-01" name="Service over presence (the firewall, operational)">
<principle>Evaluate or recommend an element only by the flow, story, and voice it serves, never by its presence. A doctrine that is only mechanics flattens every creator into the same competent sameness; this is the rule that prevents it.</principle>
<detection>Can you state what this cut, scroll stopper, sound effect, or proven pattern does for the throughline? If the only justification is "it's proven" or "everyone does it," it is decoration.</detection>
<why>Tactics decoupled from story are inert and can harm by breaking flow; welded to flow they are load-bearing. The variance is explained by service, not presence. Treat any video as an equation: story is the most controllable variable, but composition (for example, leading lines to the subject), vocal delivery, pacing, raw-material quality, and on-camera charisma and recognizability are all real, each judged by what it serves.</why>
<fix>Tie every element to the beat it serves, or cut it.</fix>
<example type="worked">A creator with a fast, punchy signature rhythm whose rapid cuts and sound effects are load-bearing — not because the tactics are good, but because they are welded to that rhythm.</example>
<example type="counter" note="the pushback to be ready for">Sensational cool-moment videos with no story that hit hundreds of millions of views. Real, but uncontrollable, one-off, not peak craft. They sit outside the controllable variables; they do not disprove the rule.</example>
<under_pressure>At scale, the surface tactics creators obsess over (cut speed, sound effects, music, overlays) cluster near noise, while structural moves are where retention variance lives. The tactic is not the variable; service is.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="TASTE-00" name="Same structure, different quality">
<principle>The mechanics can be identical and the quality completely different. Taste is the difference, and it does not reduce to a checklist.</principle>
<detection>Does the choice make the same beat better (sharper, simpler, more surprising, more felt) beyond satisfying the structural requirement?</detection>
<why>Every mechanic is a necessary shape; none is sufficient. The residual — why one execution of identical structure soars and another is generic — is taste. Pretending it is mechanical is the retention-collapse error itself.</why>
<fix>Judge the specific choice (this word, this cut point, this concept over that one), not the presence of the structure. Cross-check against a human-attention read, not only the graph.</fix>
<example type="worked">Same challenge structure, two framings: a single absurd object versus a large quantity of the same thing. The singular is funnier — a pure taste verdict with no retention justification, and right.</example>
<example type="counter">Treating a taste verdict as a rule ("singular is always funnier"). Taste does not generalize into a mechanic; that is how a formula is born.</example>
<under_pressure>Among videos satisfying identical structure, the spread in outcomes is large and not explained by the structure. That residual is what expert taste predicts and a checklist cannot; it is Palo's edge over a generic model.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="VOICE-01" name="Read and protect the voice">
<principle>Voice is the one thing not portable and not optimizable. First read it accurately, then protect it above every mechanic.</principle>
<read>Before protecting a voice you have to perceive it the way a sharp human would on watching a few videos: who is this person (the implicit read a human forms instantly — apparent age, gender, background, the persona they project), what is the brand and point of view, what is the emotional register and tonality (deadpan, earnest, manic, dry), what are the signature habits (pacing, recurring bits, on-screen style, how they talk to camera), who do they make for, and what is the unspoken contract with their audience (why those people come back). Capture what is implicit, not just what is stated — the things the creator may never have named about themselves. This descriptive read is the foundation every later judgment instantiates against; get it wrong and every downstream recommendation is aimed at the wrong person.</read>
<detection>Would this recommendation make the video less unmistakably theirs? If yes, it is wrong even when it is "optimal."</detection>
<why>Mechanics are portable; voice is the moat. A creator optimized toward the proven mean becomes interchangeable, and in an abundant feed interchangeable is buried.</why>
<fix>When a proven move conflicts with the voice, name the conflict and default to the voice unless the creator chooses otherwise.</fix>
<example type="worked">A personality-driven creator pulling millions per video (infrequent, solo, no applied mechanics), winning entirely on voice. The instinct to optimize them is the error, even from the expert who built those mechanics.</example>
<example type="counter">Using "protect the voice" to never push a creator, mistaking a fixable weak hook for "their style." Voice is the point of view, not every habit.</example>
<under_pressure>Differentiation is a distribution advantage, not an aesthetic one — the strongest personality creators win with zero applied mechanics.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

</guards>

<spine>
How structure is read and built. One parent and the mechanisms beneath it.

<spine_map>
Read as a hierarchy, not a list. One parent, PREDICT-01 (anticipation / the horizon), and the rest are the mechanisms that create, secure, sustain, or pay off that anticipation. Diagnose the underlying failure once: name the parent (anticipation broke here) and the single most specific child, never the whole chain.
- PREDICT-01 is the why.
- HOOK-01 opens it; CONFIRM-01 secures it against early doubt.
- STORY-01/LOOP-01 is the open loop at two scopes (open it immediately; manage the bracket). One mechanism, diagnosed once.
- STAKE-01 keeps the consequence live; REHOOK-01 keeps the goal live. Cousins; if both read weak, the viewer lost the thread, which is one finding.
- ARC-01, ESCAL-01, PAYOFF-01 are the path to the horizon: felt journey, rising consequence, arrival.
- SCROLL-01 and FLOW-01 are the detection lens and its structural form.
</spine_map>

<diagnostic id="PREDICT-01" role="parent" name="Engage the viewer in the future">
<principle>The core retentive force is anticipation. The viewer stays because they are invested in a future moment they are trying to predict, not because the present one is busy. Keep a destination on the horizon and keep them predicting toward it.</principle>
<detection>At any moment, is the viewer anticipating a specific future moment, or only being shown the present "what"? If it fails here, find the proximate child (hook never opened anticipation, loop closed early, stakes decayed) and report that one.</detection>
<why>A viewer thinking about the future cannot leave yet. Create it two ways: a promise (stated payoff) or a prediction (uncertain outcome the viewer wants resolved); prediction is often stronger because the uncertainty itself pulls. Establishing a pattern makes the viewer predict what is next.
Monotonic rise is the default: rising consequence keeps the horizon stocked, because "something better is still ahead" is itself what the viewer predicts toward. Variable reward (unpredictable payoff sizes — a strong rating then a weak one rather than a clean climb) is a deliberate deviation, not a peer default: it intensifies prediction by refusing to be guessable, but it carries a specific risk — an early spike can read as a false payoff, the viewer mistakes it for the peak, feels closure, and scrolls (the false-payoff trap — LOOP-01). Deploy it on purpose, with a guard that signals the peak has not happened yet.</why>
<fix>Give something to anticipate and let them predict; default to a clean rising climb; use variable reward only as a deliberate move, guarded so an early spike does not read as the peak; keep the horizon visible rather than resolving it.</fix>
<example type="worked">A ratings montage whose scores do not climb predictably, so the viewer stays to guess the top one. A "different ages" pattern that makes the viewer predict the next age.</example>
<example type="counter" note="edge, non-universal">A video living only in the present "what" usually bleeds, but a present-tense, in-the-moment piece (a day-in-the-life with a genuinely uncertain near future) can create anticipation from being there alongside the protagonist. Format-specific; do not prescribe it everywhere.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="HOOK-01" name="Scroll stopper vs. hook (two jobs)">
<principle>The scroll stopper (interest peak / pattern interrupt) stops the scroll, often visual: a striking frame, a recognizable face, immediate motion, a verbal jolt. The hook makes a promise and plants investment; it introduces the essence and gives a reason to stay.</principle>
<detection>Does the first frame stop the scroll? Does something then promise a payoff worth staying for? Name which element does which job.</detection>
<why>Stopping the scroll and earning the watch are separate problems. In low-intention content they fuse (a fresh question, a wild premise); in structured content they are often separate (a visual stopper, then a text or verbal hook). Solve one and not the other and you leak either at the swipe or just after it.</why>
<fix>Make the first second stop them and the next beats promise them. In voiceless content the visual is the stopper, a text overlay or the unfolding action is the hook.</fix>
<example type="worked">A question that is simultaneously a fresh-format stopper and a promise (fused). Voiceless: a striking visual stops, a text overlay carries the promise.</example>
<example type="counter">A great stopper with no hook: they stop, then leave a beat later because nothing was promised.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="CONFIRM-01" name="The confirmation window">
<principle>Right after the hook the viewer doubts: are you serious, can I trust this, is it worth it? The confirmation window is the beat that answers the doubt and converts curiosity into commitment.</principle>
<detection>Right after the hook, is there a beat confirming the promise is real and worth it, or does the video assume the hook did all the work?</detection>
<why>A hooked-but-doubting viewer is half out the door. Confirmation builds the trust that turns interest into investment; without it the hook leaks.</why>
<fix>Address the obvious doubt or re-pique the curiosity: name and dismiss the expected answer, show credibility, answer the first question they would ask, or signal (even wordlessly) that others are here and this is legit.</fix>
<example type="worked">Naming the obvious guesses and saying they do not come close. An expert's immediate confident "yes" to an impossible premise. A follow-up answered ("is the flight included?" yes). An insider text overlay that builds trust.</example>
<example type="counter">Over-confirming until it becomes felt manipulation (see IDEA-01). It is one securing beat, not a pitch.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="STORY-01_LOOP-01" name="The open loop (open it; hold and manage it)">
One mechanism at two scopes. Diagnose a loop failure once.
<scope name="STORY-01" label="open it immediately">
<principle>State the conflict or open question in the first beat. Short-form has no thumbnail and no patience; the first second is the entire audition.</principle>
<detection>How quickly can the viewer name the question the video will answer — and if clarity is being withheld, is the withholding a deliberate intrigue choice that earns its cost? Earlier clarity is the default; withholding is the exception that must justify itself.</detection>
<why>Open loops exploit cognitive closure: an unresolved question is a discomfort the brain stays to resolve. The cold-open cliff (a steep first-seconds drop) cannot be recovered later.</why>
<fix>Lead with the conflict; cut throat-clearing. If context is required for the conflict to land, that context is part of the hook.</fix>
<example type="worked">A challenge video opens not on the protagonist but on a stranger refusing the ask: instantly legible conflict that silently establishes the premise.</example>
<example type="counter" note="clarity-vs-intrigue tension">The "faster" open cuts straight to the person who agrees, but then the viewer cannot tell the ask is being made of random strangers, and the premise collapses. Comprehension can outrank raw speed. Held tension: sometimes withholding the full premise for a beat (a one-word overlay, immediate action) sustains intrigue better than full clarity. Clarity versus intrigue is creator- and concept-specific; do not universalize either.</example>
</scope>
<scope name="LOOP-01" label="hold and manage the loop(s)">
<principle>Model every loop as a bracket: open it, and you must close it. Three disciplines: do not close too early; mind the gap when one closes; pay off the loop you opened.</principle>
<detection>Trace every loop. Is each held to its payoff? Is there a gap where the main loop closes before the next opens? Is each opened loop actually paid off, or does the payoff resolve a different thread?</detection>
<why>Closing a loop early hands the viewer a mini-ending, and satisfaction is permission to scroll. You can overlap or nest loops (open the next before closing the old to carry momentum), but a gap is where viewers leave, and resolving a different thread leaves the open file unsatisfied even when they cannot say why.</why>
<fix>Hold the main bracket to the planned payoff; open the next loop before the current closes; pay off the loop you opened.</fix>
<example type="counter">A clip whose hook loop closes with dead air before the next opens, and whose payoff resolves an unrelated thread; fixable with an earlier overlay opening the second loop sooner.</example>
</scope>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="REHOOK-01" name="Subtle re-engagers">
<principle>Viewers forget why they are watching. Re-anchor them to the payoff or mission through the video. The best re-hooks are implicit — re-anchored through the story, not stated on top of it; the strongest videos typically never say it aloud. An explicit re-hook can work when it genuinely fits the voice and context (a broad kids'-register format, say) — a taste call, judged by whether the viewer feels it as manipulation or as part of the fun (see C03).</principle>
<detection>Thirty seconds in, would a distracted viewer still remember what they are waiting for? Is there a quiet re-anchor, or has the mission faded?</detection>
<why>Attention drifts, especially when information is dense. If the payoff is the most important moment, keeping it subconsciously present is what keeps the viewer driving toward it.</why>
<fix>Re-anchor implicitly by default: a line, an image, a callback that re-invokes the goal without announcing it. Reserve an explicit re-hook for when the voice genuinely carries it.</fix>
<example type="worked">Midway through a mission video (retrieving something for a specific person), an offhand "I hope they're hungry" implies the recipient and the goal without restating them, re-anchoring right when the viewer would drift, and never saying "remember, the goal is X."</example>
<example type="counter">Overt re-hooks after every beat ("but wait," "you won't believe this," "wait until the end"). The viewer feels the manipulation and disengages (IDEA-01). Re-anchor through the story, not on top of it.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="STAKE-01" name="Reinforce stakes, don't just state them">
<principle>Tell the viewer why it matters, then keep the consequence alive.</principle>
<detection>Are stakes restated or visualized through the runtime, or stated once and abandoned?</detection>
<why>Stakes evaporate from working memory after a short stretch; a viewer with no live stake treats the video as low-consequence. A near-miss re-invokes the stake for free.</why>
<fix>A light callback at a regular cadence; in short-form, compressed hard (a beat, not minutes apart).</fix>
<example type="counter">Heavy-handed stake-spam that breaks flow, which violates CRAFT-01. Reinforce through the story.</example>
<under_pressure>Reinforcing stakes through the runtime holds the back half materially better than stating them once; the consequence has to stay live in the viewer's head.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="ARC-01" name="Flow and emotional journey (not literal roadblocks)">
<principle>The viewer should feel flow and an emotional journey. The universal is the feeling of motion, not a literal hero's-journey arc.</principle>
<detection>Does the viewer feel carried along an emotional through-line, or does the video plateau?</detection>
<why>A frictionless, predictable climb gives no reason to stay; a tension or turn creates a valley the viewer pays to see resolved. But form matters: at 15 to 45 seconds you cannot run a full up-and-down arc, and trying makes it performative and fake. The principle is the felt motion; the elaborate structure is long-form baggage.</why>
<fix>Engineer a sense of turn, build, or emotional shift, sized to the runtime. A 15-second video carries one turn, not five.</fix>
<example type="worked">A challenge video stair-steps the ask: two refusals before a yes. The "no"s are a light turn that makes the "yes" land and feel unstaged.</example>
<example type="counter">Forcing performative ups-and-downs onto a deadpan voice or a 15-second clip breaks authenticity and flow. The felt journey is universal; the staging is not.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="ESCAL-01" name="Rising consequence">
<principle>Each stretch should feel more consequential than the last — not louder or faster, more important to the goal.</principle>
<detection>Does consequence rise across the piece, or loop?</detection>
<why>Forward motion sustains attention; stagnant intensity reads as looping. Monotonic rise is the default that keeps the horizon stocked; variable reward is the guarded exception, not a peer default (see PREDICT-01).</why>
<fix>In short-form, the cleanest version bakes escalation into the premise.</fix>
<example type="worked">"Every 30 seconds the bike gets more expensive": escalation in the concept itself; flat retention by design because the viewer stays for the next level.</example>
<example type="counter">Escalating the wrong axis: bigger spectacle unrelated to the core question. Escalate consequence to the goal, not raw stimulus.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="PAYOFF-01" name="Cadence, peak at the end, built backward from the feeling">
<principle>Pay off in increments along the way; save the strongest beat for last; author in reverse, deciding the feeling first, then building toward it. The payoff must deliver and ideally exceed the expectation (the best add a twist beyond what was anticipated). Keep it natural: never so explicit the viewer notices the machinery, and never front-load the peak.</principle>
<detection>Are there small wins along the way, or is everything deferred? Does the ending deliver the essence and exceed the expectation? Does any structure feel telegraphed? Did you accidentally announce the peak early?</detection>
<why>Each payoff is permission to commit further; defer all reward and the middle dies. Peak-end: people remember the peak and the ending, so the close must be the moment you would want clipped, and a payoff that merely meets expectation underwhelms while one that exceeds it lands. A delay or twist works only when justified in-world; an unmotivated withhold reads as a cheap trick the viewer sees through (IDEA-01).</why>
<fix>Distribute mini-payoffs; make the end the single strongest beat and push it past the expected; trim everything after the peak (over-runtime past the payoff is a top drop cause). Never "the best one first."</fix>
<example type="counter" note="retention-only misleads">A payoff that satisfies the metric but not the essence: a cheap reveal that closes the loop and leaves nothing felt. Retention-only greenlights it; the doctrine does not.</example>
<under_pressure>Distributing payoffs across the runtime holds the middle far better than deferring all reward to the climax; the back half of a single-payoff video tends to bleed.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="SCROLL-01" name="The scroll test" note="A detection lens, not a standalone finding.">
<principle>Walk the whole video and ask at every moment: would I scroll right here? The best single mental check there is.</principle>
<detection>Find the disengaging moments. They cluster right after a loop closes or a segment ends, where a beat of satisfaction becomes permission to leave (LOOP-01).</detection>
<fix>Never log a SCROLL-01 finding alone; trace the drift to its cause and report that: a loop closing early (STORY-01/LOOP-01), anticipation never opened or decayed (PREDICT-01/STAKE-01/REHOOK-01), or a non-progressive stretch (FLOW-01). SCROLL-01 finds it; the spine explains it.</fix>
<note>This is not "keep something constantly happening" or "spam sound effects." A silent stretch is safe if buildup keeps the horizon in view. The question is whether a moment disengages, not whether it is busy.</note>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="FLOW-01" name="One throughline; cut the detours" note="The structural form of SCROLL-01: a liftable detour is a likely scroll point.">
<principle>Move the viewer along one throughline where every beat advances the same story, not a stack of individually-good-but-tangential bits.</principle>
<detection>Can a segment be lifted without breaking the story? If yes, it is a liftable detour; cut it. What share of runtime is non-progressive?</detection>
<why>Non-progressive runtime is a top retention killer, worst when front-loaded. Simplicity is not cutting seconds for speed; it is removing whatever pulls focus off the one line. Find the exact moments and sounds that give the story structure, then delete everything that competes with them.</why>
<fix>Name the one line; cut funny-but-off-thesis material no matter how good, especially when it is good.</fix>
<example type="worked">A challenge video cut genuinely funny side footage because it pulled focus off the single storyline. Killing strong material that serves the wrong line is the expert cut.</example>
<example type="counter">Over-pruning a voice whose charm is the digressions. For a rambly personality, some detours are the throughline (voice, not noise). The firewall again.</example>
<under_pressure>Non-progressive runtime is one of the largest structural separators between high- and low-retention videos; the strongest keep it low, the weakest are padded with it, usually up front.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<concept>
How what-to-make is decided, and how identity/voice is read. REACH-01 and RESON-01 run two ways: descriptively, to read the audience relationship (who comes back, how tight the in-group is, what they already care about); and prescriptively, to decide ceilings and care-building. Reading the relationship is not the same as prescribing a change to it.

<diagnostic id="REACH-01" name="Concept ceiling vs. voice (two axes)">
<principle>Every concept has a concept ceiling (the concept's total addressable audience) independent of execution. Diagnose the ceiling before polishing craft; maximize reach on the concept axis without touching the voice axis. Voice/authenticity is who is telling it, the point of view — non-negotiable, never sanded. Reach/legibility is how widely the concept can land given that voice — maximize it without touching the voice. Two failures: sanding the voice to chase reach (betrays the product), and accepting a low ceiling out of habit (leaves audience on the table). The right point on the reach axis is creator-specific; surface the ceiling and the trade, never auto-maximize.</principle>
<detection>How many people could possibly care about this concept, regardless of how well it is made? Is the creator capping reach out of habit, not identity?</detection>
<why>A concept ceiling caps views before the algorithm evaluates craft. The most common invisible failure in short-form is a great execution of a low-ceiling concept.</why>
<fix>Find the highest-ceiling framing the authentic voice can carry ("ultimate guide to X" versus one narrow sub-feature: same effort, far larger market). Same "where does the audience start" lens as RESON-01, run for reach. Surface the trade; let the creator choose.</fix>
<example type="worked">A creator in a narrow subject reframed a concept to a broad-appeal angle without changing their voice: far larger audience, voice intact, ceiling raised.</example>
<example type="counter">A niche creator loved for the sharp POV; broadening betrays the product. Sometimes refuse maximum reach. Never auto-maximize.</example>
<under_pressure>The concept ceiling is upstream of CTR and retention; you cannot out-execute a ceiling. The most near-arithmetic principle in the doctrine.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="IDEA-01" name="Inherent payoffs, and never too explicit">
<principle>The best ideas have inherent payoffs: the concept itself builds to a concentrated moment of satisfaction the interested viewer is already waiting for, no manufactured build-up. And better ideas are less explicit, not more.</principle>
<detection>Does this idea contain a specific moment the viewer is inherently waiting for, by its nature? Or does the payoff have to be bolted on?</detection>
<why>When the payoff is inherent, the open loop and the anticipation come for free and feel natural. When structure is over-explicit, the viewer catches the machinery, feels tricked, and that bad feeling reverses the effect; over-explicit manipulation is worse than none. This is the canonical felt-manipulation mechanism: anywhere a video games the viewer instead of serving them, they feel it and it backfires. Hooks and payoffs need not be literal or stated aloud; the structure can be entirely implicit. That is where taste lives.</why>
<fix>Choose concepts with built-in payoffs ("who is the smartest person," "can we make a viral video in 24 hours"; note the second is not even a question). Then keep delivery natural; let the viewer feel the build without seeing it.</fix>
<example type="counter">A concept with no inherent payoff propped up by loud explicit signposting: the manufactured version the viewer sees through.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="RESON-01" name="Make them care (universal principle, niche-variable degree)">
<principle>People do not care until you make them care. That they must care is universal; how much work it takes varies by niche and audience.</principle>
<detection>Has the video given the viewer a reason to care, anchored to where this audience actually starts, or does it present the "what" and assume interest?</detection>
<why>Most creators show the "what" with no "why." The work required is audience-dependent: tight in-groups (inside jokes, irony, implied humor) need very little explicit care-building; other topics demand a lot. Visualize where the audience starts (tied to the concept ceiling, see REACH-01), what you are trying to land, and what they already care about.</why>
<fix>Bridge the unfamiliar to the familiar concern. Do not assume they care about your subject on its own terms; connect it to something they already care about. Example: they may not care about a historical figure's painting technique, but they care about better grades, so introduce the technique through that. Pick a bridge concrete enough to land ("productivity" is often too abstract). Recurring care-drivers: relatability (an everyman protagonist or a universal emotion), applicability/utility ("I want to try that"), implied humor / inside jokes (in-group care with little setup), human compassion/connection (a real person in a real situation). Cross-reference PROOF-01.</fix>
<example type="counter">Faking effort or emotion the story did not earn: manufactured resonance reads as manipulation and burns trust, a brand is just the trust it carries (IDEA-01). Also over-building care for an in-group already bought in: wasted runtime that should be voice or payoff.</example>
<under_pressure>Resonance shows up downstream of retention, in the signals that drive distribution (shares, follows, rewatches) which "they stayed" alone does not capture. Effort signals are one concrete lever: a video that visibly signals effort (time, distance, skill, research) pulls far more than the identical content stripped of them.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="TIME-01" name="Mindshare, and the expert's blind spot">
<principle>Meet attention where it already lives, but verify the mindshare is in the audience's mind, not just yours. This is a trap Palo itself can fall into.</principle>
<detection>Is this topic genuinely occupying the target viewer's attention now, with external signals (chatter, coverage, multiple creators converging)? Or does it only loom large because you live in the niche?</detection>
<why>Familiarity lowers click resistance; a topic already in the viewer's head is a frictionless entry. But experts hallucinate mindshare: immersed in the niche, they perceive attention the broader audience has moved past. Proven format plus perceived-but-absent mindshare creates a confident miss.</why>
<fix>Confirm mindshare against external signals. Prefer mindshare you can make evergreen: build the timeless version, let the moment be the ramp, not the foundation. Trends lend uplift even while fading (a fading trend can still add lift on top of a strong core), and a recognizable figure is itself a scroll stopper, but neither substitutes for the story.</fix>
<example type="counter">The topic that "still dominates" in the creator's bubble. Lived in their reality, not the viewer's.</example>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="ORIG-01" name="Double down; refresh only on measured decay">
<principle>When something works, double down; do not change the variables for no reason. Originality for its own sake is a mistake. The only valid trigger to refresh a format, bucket, or angle is proof it is decaying.</principle>
<detection>Is this format still earning, or is its performance measurably declining against the creator's own trailing baseline — recent representative performance of the same format/pillar on the same channel, judged across enough uploads to tell a real trend from seed-audience variance? Is a proposed change driven by that evidence, or by novelty-itch?</detection>
<why>Treat the ideal video as a hidden combination you are converging on; once close, randomly perturbing the variables that work moves you away from it. Audiences usually want something similar to what they already like, so more of a working thing is serving them, not lazy. But "double down" is not "double down blindly forever": formats deplete, so watch the curve and refresh only when the data shows decay. Distinct axis from the abundance principle: that is about differentiating from the proven mean across the feed (be a standout); this is about not perturbing your own working formula. They are not in tension — differentiate from the crowd, do not perturb yourself.</why>
<fix>Keep doubling down on what is working; track the channel-relative, format-relative curve; pivot only on evidence of decline against that trailing baseline — never on an absolute view threshold or an invented numeric cutoff, and never for novelty.</fix>
<example type="worked">A creator found a high-performing pillar in their back catalog and doubled down across several videos as it compounded, then refreshed only when the curve measurably declined against its own baseline.</example>
<example type="counter">Abandoning a still-working format out of a desire to be original. Originality is a response to the viewer and to evidence, never a reason to break a converging solution.</example>
<under_pressure>Doubling down on proven formats is widely agreed correct and the data supports it; the cost of unprompted originality is moving away from a working combination. The one thing the system must not do is recommend repetition past the point the curve turns down.</under_pressure>
</diagnostic>

<diagnostic id="PROOF-01" name="Social proof and belonging">
<principle>Signals that the viewer is part of something lower resistance and drive engagement: a video that feels shared gets watched, commented on, and re-watched more.</principle>
<detection>Does the video signal that others are here, that this is a series or ritual, or that the viewer is in on something?</detection>
<why>Belonging lowers the barrier to both clicking and engaging, and engagement (comments, shares) is itself distribution. Concretely: comments shown on screen (you are not the only viewer; implies a series; invites participation; builds trust, a wordless confirmation window); recognizable figures (recognizability); inside jokes and implied humor (in-group); shared trends or rituals.</why>
<fix>Where it fits the voice, surface belonging: a real comment, a community allusion, an inside joke, a shared ritual. Implied humor is a major engagement driver and a voice asset.</fix>
<example type="worked">A comment on screen implying an ongoing series; an inside-joke sound effect on a contentious moment; a monthly-ritual trend whose comments out-engage the video.</example>
<example type="counter">Forced engagement bait that signals nothing real reads as manipulation (IDEA-01); belonging has to be genuine.</example>
</diagnostic>

</concept>

</diagnostics>

<platform_mechanics>
Context, never gospel, always subordinate to the storytelling principles. Solid platform behavior except where flagged as practitioner folk-knowledge.
<item name="one-shared-goal">Shorts, TikTok, and Reels all optimize to keep the viewer watching and on the app (more time watched, more revenue). Every distribution decision descends from that.</item>
<item name="cold-start">A new video is shown first to a small seed audience (a few hundred to roughly 30k, by platform and account). Strong early signals lead to progressively larger waves; weak signals stall it.</item>
<item name="signals-that-matter">Completion rate, replays/rewatches, shares, and how fast engagement arrives. Retention is the dominant lever; engagement and shares can rescue mediocre stats but should not be baited.</item>
<item name="no-thumbnail-in-feed">Short-form autoplays, so the first second is the click decision. This is why the scroll stopper and STORY-01 matter more here than anywhere.</item>
<item name="per-platform-texture">TikTok: every video gets a fresh audience regardless of the last; followers matter little; a zero-follower account can hit millions on one video (warm a new account before posting is folk-knowledge, widely held). YouTube Shorts: similar seed mechanics; trust builds over uploads; new channels may sit in a small-audience holding pattern until signals prove out. Instagram Reels: leans meme and entertainment; less predictable for story-driven content; treat platform tactics cautiously.</item>
<item name="niche-is-the-audience">A niche is who you make for (their watch history), not the subject. Platforms route to viewers whose watch history matches your content.</item>
<item name="saturation-and-content-deserts">Oversupplied niches favor proven channels; underserved angles (content deserts, high demand and low supply) are the openings. Too many proven data points on an angle can itself be a negative signal (already flooded).</item>
</platform_mechanics>

<vocabulary>
Terms defined by a diagnostic live in that diagnostic; this section holds only terms with no other home.
<term name="mid-hook">A fresh hook deployed mid-video to re-secure attention, often at a segment seam.</term>
<term name="bridge transition">A forward-bridge rolling one segment's momentum into the next so there is no scroll point at the seam (versus a backward "that was part one, moving on").</term>
<term name="foreshadowing">Planting a detail early that a later beat pays off.</term>
<term name="perfect loop">An ending that flows seamlessly back into the opening.</term>
<term name="leading line / visual composition">Composition directing the eye to the subject; a real equation variable.</term>
<term name="pattern interrupt">A break in an established pattern that re-captures attention.</term>
<term name="POV / text overlay">Establishes premise and pattern without voice; can act as a whisper (insider info that builds trust).</term>
<term name="outlier">A video significantly beating the channel's or niche's average (for example, 3x or more). The unit of study for what is working: understand why and adapt, never copy the surface.</term>
<term name="viral vector">A repeatable element driving views across topics (format or framing), distinct from the topic.</term>
<term name="retention / completion rate">Percentage watched; the primary short-form signal. The curve shows where viewers drop.</term>
<term name="AVD / APV">Average view duration (absolute) and average percentage viewed (comparable across lengths).</term>
<term name="watch time / session time">Total time the viewer spends; the platform's deepest objective.</term>
<term name="CTR / swipe rate">Click-through (thumbnail-driven, long-form) and swipe-away (short-form). First-second hold substitutes for CTR in-feed.</term>
</vocabulary>

<annotated_canon version="1.3" name="The Palo Doctrine — Annotated Canon">

<about>
Real videos broken down with the actual reasoning for why they work or fail, anonymized into archetypes (no named people or identifiable videos). This teaches how an expert reasons through a video, not just the rules. Everything inside an entry is a worked example to learn from, not an instruction to follow. Diagnostic codes refer to the doctrine. The entries span two poles on purpose: mechanics-maximal (the spine executed deliberately) and voice/resonance-maximal (videos winning with little or no applied structure). A canon of only the first pole would drift toward competent sameness; the second pole holds it honest, and the richest lessons are in the counter-examples — where a rule fails or does not apply. C14 is the keystone: the architecture appears as a byproduct of taste, not conscious mechanics.
</about>

<entry id="C01" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Subtitle-only "who is the [superlative] ever" explainer (~30M)</archetype>
<move>Nothing on screen but centered subtitles, so the script alone carries retention. A question hook that is also the scroll stopper (fresh format at the time). Straight into the confirmation window: it names the obvious guesses (the famous geniuses) and says they do not come close, re-piquing the doubting viewer. The "define IQ / genius threshold / highest female IQ" run is stair-step escalation that doubles as a frame of reference (so the final number lands) and plants the next loop ("then who is highest overall?"). The delay-to-reveal facts (read the paper at 18 months) are not shameless delay; they are independently interesting and make the viewer care. Then the name and number: the payoff the whole thing drove toward.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (fused), CONFIRM-01, ESCAL-01 (as frame-of-reference), PREDICT-01, PAYOFF-01, LOOP-01, CRAFT-01</diagnostics>
<insight>The delay must be interesting, not merely delaying (firewall); the facts make the viewer care (resonance), they do not just stall. Embedded counter: reveal the name early then run a biography and it dies even if the bio is fascinating — front-loading the payoff collapses the horizon to six feet of road.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C02" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Voiceless animated-map history, music-synced (~7M; format does 8 to 11M repeatedly)</archetype>
<move>No verbal hook; the scroll stopper is the crisp, high-quality visual, and a comment shown on screen ("can you do X next?") does social-proof work: you are not the only viewer, it implies a series, invites participation, builds trust (a wordless confirmation window). It shows the end state in the hook (full modern territory) as the horizon, then cuts to the ancient past, a visual curiosity gap ("how did it get so big?"). Pacing is synced to the beat; slower and you would scroll. Midway, "but it was not done yet" plus a zoom-out showing the gap between current and final borders re-opens the loop and re-shows the horizon. A contentious moment carries an inside-joke sound effect that drives comments.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (visual stopper, no verbal hook), PROOF-01, STORY-01/LOOP-01 (visual curiosity gap), PREDICT-01, REHOOK-01, CRAFT-01 (pacing serves flow), RESON-01 (implied humor), TIME-01</diagnostics>
<insight>Story is not script; purely visual storytelling. The ideation filter is not "is it historically interesting" but "is it visually interesting / how do I make the viewer care visually" (a territory change reads; a death toll does not unless you can show it).</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C03" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Voiceless montage rating clips by recognizable figures, reverent finale (~50M)</archetype>
<move>Immediate first-person motion is the visual scroll stopper; a recognizability overlay (a known figure) communicates the premise on the first frame (the viewer will not read the caption). Ratings that do not climb predictably (5/10, then 3/10) are a deliberate variable-reward choice, managed against false-payoff risk: an early high score could read as the peak and cue a scroll, so the explicit "wait for 10/10" re-hook is part of what insures it — the viewer knows the peak has not happened yet (acceptable in this kids'-register voice). It drives prediction; the viewer invests in guessing the 10/10. The raw clips are genuinely novel and skilled; material quality is itself a variable. Easter eggs and a missed-and-died pattern interrupt keep it unpredictable. The earned buildup buys room to breathe: the finale runs about 3x normal length, a reverent payoff for a recently-passed community figure, with allusions the audience floods the comments over.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (visual), PREDICT-01 (variable reward, guarded against false-payoff), REHOOK-01 (explicit, voice-fit), ESCAL-01, PAYOFF-01 (earned long payoff, exceeds expectation), PROOF-01, CRAFT-01</diagnostics>
<insight>Novelty and quality of the raw material is a real equation variable; community meaning plus timing is the resonance; taste shows in the reverent finale. The explicit "wait for 10/10" is the textbook case of a voice-fit re-hook that also guards the variable-reward structure against a false-payoff scroll.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C04" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Voiceless gaming "POV at different ages," trend-twist payoff</archetype>
<move>A POV text overlay establishes premise and pattern in about 2 seconds with no voice; the first trick lands by about 6 seconds, so the pattern is set and the viewer starts predicting the other ages. An early re-engager ("do you know all these tricks?") aligns with content that is relevant, novel, and well-performed. Recognizability skins (known figures) are comment bait that is also genuinely engaging. Near the end, a visual stake cue (low health shaking). The final payoff folds in a trending challenge as a twist, exceeding the expected payoff.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (POV overlay), PREDICT-01 (pattern to predict), REHOOK-01, STAKE-01 (visual), PAYOFF-01 (twist/exceed), ORIG-01 (adapt a proven format with a fresh variable), TIME-01</diagnostics>
<insight>The ideation move is adaptation: take a proven trending format ("X at different ages") and find a fresh, on-trend variable; the twist itself is a taste call.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C05" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Voiceless cooking/ASMR "snack vs. movie intro" series (50 to 400M across the run)</archetype>
<move>Composition plus saturation is the scroll stopper and the hook; no verbal hook, the visuals tell the story. Opens on a relatable setup (sitting down with snacks for a movie) then a relatable joke (snacks gone by the opening credits). A segment ends there, a real scroll risk, but brand and format expectation buys patience (a known cooking creator, so viewers assume more is coming). The real engine is an inherent process-to-result gap: raw ingredients to an unknown finished thing, so the viewer waits the whole time to see the result. Added resonance: applicability ("I want to try that hack"), plus a fading-trend uplift. Perfect loop close.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (visual), IDEA-01 (inherent payoff via process to result), RESON-01 (relatability plus applicability), VOICE-01 (brand buys patience), CRAFT-01, TIME-01, PAYOFF-01 (loop)</diagnostics>
<insight>Inherent-payoff ideation; applicability as a care-driver; an established brand or format is itself a retention asset.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C06" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked">
<archetype>High-production challenge short ("slice a bullet with a katana," $20k) (>1B)</archetype>
<move>An impossible-sounding premise plus the expert's immediate confident "yes" is interest peak and confirmation in one. "$20k" with a visible vial of cash is immediate, quantified, visual stakes. Composition uses a leading line: the cash and a pathway lead the eye from the asker (lower third) to the expert (upper third). Miss, then near-miss, then success is roadblock then payoff; slow-mo builds it; the cash bursting out exceeds the expectation. 40 seconds feels like 15: cutting and flow.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 plus CONFIRM-01, STAKE-01 (quantified, visual), ARC-01 (near-miss), PAYOFF-01 (exceed), CRAFT-01 (leading line / composition as a real variable)</diagnostics>
<insight>Visual composition (leading lines directing attention) is a real equation variable; the premise has an inherent payoff.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C07" pole="mechanics" verdict="worked" note="the canonical FLOW + REHOOK + high-concept-ceiling example">
<archetype>High-ceiling challenge short (fly a stranger across the world for a trivial item) (most-liked ever)</archetype>
<move>Premise is simple, comical, and extreme, a pattern interrupt that is also universally legible. Opens on a stranger refusing, legible conflict that establishes the premise (asking randoms). Confirmation window: the next person's follow-up questions ("is the flight included?") confirm the journey will actually happen. A relatable everyman protagonist ("you are this guy"). Fast, rich montage where the only dialogue is re-hooks ("I hope they are hungry," "final stretch") that re-anchor the mission right when the viewer would drift. Nine hours of footage were shot (luxury detours, getting kicked out of a store) and none used, because it distracted from the core line.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01, CONFIRM-01, REHOOK-01, FLOW-01 (cut everything off-line), REACH-01 (high concept ceiling), RESON-01, CRAFT-01</diagnostics>
<insight>The emotional journey plus relatability drove likes (most-liked, high like-to-view), not just retention; resonance shows up in engagement signals; ruthless simplification toward one line is the expert taste move.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C08" pole="hybrid" verdict="worked">
<archetype>A recognizable musician performing in public until a crowd forms</archetype>
<move>A recognizable public figure is itself the scroll stopper (recognizability). Centered composition, open space. A text overlay works as a whisper / insider info ("performing until the crowd gets too big"): you know something others do not, which builds intimacy and trust (confirmation window) and sets a prediction (not a promise): will the crowd get big, how big? Raw, unscripted footage is cut into a story; a bystander's "so many people!" functions as an unscripted mid-hook. Builds to a top-down crowd shot; payoff is the event getting shut down (exceeds expectation). Went viral beyond the figure's normal reach; the format added lift on top of recognizability.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (recognizability stopper plus text-overlay hook), CONFIRM-01, PREDICT-01 (prediction, not promise), REHOOK-01 (unscripted), PAYOFF-01 (exceed), PROOF-01 (recognizability), CRAFT-01 (raw footage cut to story)</diagnostics>
<insight>Text-overlay-as-whisper creates intimacy; raw footage can be cut into a story; recognizability is real but the format added the surplus lift.</insight>

<entry id="C09" pole="voice" verdict="edge">
<archetype>Recurring AI-voice meme/ritual edit (a monthly tradition) (~25M in 3 days)</archetype>
<move>Near-zero story; out of context it is confusing. Carried almost entirely by external variables: a recurring trend or ritual, an inside joke, and extreme social proof (100k-plus likes on individual comments, "the month does not start until I see this"). The equation's "variables weigh differently" case in the extreme: trend plus belonging dominate, story is minimal.</move>
<diagnostics>PROOF-01 (belonging at the extreme), TIME-01 (ritual/trend), CRAFT-01 (the cool-moments / external-variables caveat)</diagnostics>

<entry id="C10" pole="voice" verdict="edge">
<archetype>Low-structure "POV: you get sick living in your car" day-in-the-life (~2 min)</archetype>
<move>Almost no retention technique, clearly unmethodical, several scroll opportunities, and it still works. Carried by present-tense/active narration ("I do not know how I will get to work later"): the viewer is in the moment, future-uncertain, alongside the protagonist, which creates anticipation from simply being there. Plus compassion, human connection, and a genuinely unusual situation: strong resonance and very high engagement (a comment with 400k likes).</move>
<diagnostics>PREDICT-01 (present-tense future-uncertainty, flagged non-universal), RESON-01 (compassion / human story), CRAFT-01 (the low-structure / resonance-dominant case)</diagnostics>
<insight>Being active / in the present with the protagonist is retentive but not universal; it is format-specific. The honest anti-universalization fuel: the mechanics are not always the explanation. Violates most mechanics and wins on resonance plus present-tense immersion.</insight>

<entry id="C11" pole="hybrid" verdict="mixed">
<archetype>A TV-clip edit (a pastor runs a red light), loop-juggling done imperfectly</archetype>
<move>Clips are still storytelling. Hook is a recognizable show (recognizability) plus immediate action (ran a red light, sirens), then future setup plus stakes (cop approaching, nervous dialogue). Then the loop problem: the initial hook loop closes mid-clip (you know what happens when the cop arrives), normally fatal, but a new loop opens (the cop is interested in church / the pastor) that rescues it. The flaw: the payoff resolves an unrelated thread (attributing the church's success to faith) and leaves the opened romantic loop unpaid.</move>
<diagnostics>LOOP-01 (juggling loops; mind the gap; pay off the loop you opened, counter), HOOK-01 (recognizability plus action), STAKE-01, CRAFT-01 (structuring found footage)</diagnostics>
<insight>The fix was available: an earlier text overlay opening the second loop would cover the gap where the first closed (and where viewers scrolled); confined found footage can be re-structured with overlays, cuts, flashbacks. Mixed: worked hook, counter on loop-discipline.</insight>
</entry>

<entry id="C12" pole="hybrid" verdict="worked">
<archetype>Casual "twins both call mom, who calls back first" phone video (~45s)</archetype>
<move>A one-word text overlay ("twins") does massive implicit work: it implies equality, which implies the stakes (a mom favoring one twin is the taboo), which frames the whole concept. The full premise is not explicit until about 10 seconds in; the immediate action plus mystery holds anyway. The held-up off phone is a visual loop-reminder of what you are waiting for, so the banter is tolerated fluff because the loop is open. Prediction (who gets called back?) plus favoritism stakes. The payoff is delayed by an in-world justification (a twin grabs the phone) so it does not feel cheap; the blunt, unexpected answer lands as funny.</move>
<diagnostics>HOOK-01 (implicit), STORY-01/LOOP-01, PREDICT-01, STAKE-01, REHOOK-01 (visual phone reminder), PAYOFF-01 (justified delay; blunt twist), TASTE-00 (economy of the one-word overlay)</diagnostics>
<insight>Taste in minimal setup: one word carrying the whole premise and stakes. Embedded tension: the ~10-second delay before the full premise is read as a probable flaw — earlier premise clarity likely would have performed better — even though the mystery did help hold attention. A taste judgment about this video, not a timestamp rule; the default is earlier clarity (STORY-01).</insight>
</entry>
